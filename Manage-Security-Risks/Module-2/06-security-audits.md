# Security Audits

## What is a Security Audit?

A **security audit** is a systematic review of an organization's **security controls, policies, and procedures** against defined standards and expectations.

### Purpose

- Evaluate security controls
- Identify risks and vulnerabilities
- Verify compliance with regulations
- Improve the organization's security posture
- Reduce the risk of data breaches and penalties

---

# Types of Security Audits

## 1. Internal Audit

Conducted by the organization's own security team.

Typical participants:

- Compliance Officer
- Security Manager
- Security Analysts
- IT Team

### Purpose

- Improve internal security
- Identify security gaps
- Ensure compliance
- Prepare for external audits

---

## 2. External Audit

Conducted by an independent third party or regulatory organization.

### Purpose

- Verify regulatory compliance
- Validate security practices
- Meet legal or industry requirements

---

# Goals of a Security Audit

The main goal is to ensure the organization's IT and security practices meet:

- Organizational policies
- Industry standards
- Security frameworks
- Compliance regulations

### Objectives

- Identify security weaknesses
- Recommend improvements
- Reduce organizational risk
- Strengthen security posture

---

# Factors That Affect Audits

Audit requirements depend on:

- Industry type
- Organization size
- Government regulations
- Geographic location
- Compliance requirements (e.g., GDPR, PCI DSS, HIPAA)

To review common compliance regulations that different organizations need to adhere to, refer to [the reading about controls, frameworks, and compliance](https://www.coursera.org/learn/foundations-of-cybersecurity/supplement/xu4pr/controls-frameworks-and-compliance).

# Role of Frameworks and Controls

Security frameworks help organizations prepare for audits.

Examples:

- NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)
- ISO/IEC 27001

Security controls are evaluated during audits to ensure they effectively protect organizational assets.

# Categories of Security Controls

## 1. Administrative Controls

Human-focused controls that define how security is managed.

Examples:

- Security policies
- Password policies
- Employee training
- Separation of duties
- Incident response procedures

---

## 2. Technical Controls

Technology-based safeguards.

Examples:

- Firewalls
- Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS)
- Encryption
- Antivirus software
- Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)
- SIEM systems

---

## 3. Physical Controls

Protect physical assets from unauthorized access.

Examples:

- Locks
- CCTV cameras
- Security guards
- Motion detectors
- Access cards
- Fences

---

# Common Elements of an Internal Security Audit

## 1. Establish Scope and Goals

### Scope

Defines what will be audited.

May include:

- People
- Assets
- Systems
- Policies
- Procedures
- Technologies

### Goals

Define what the organization wants to achieve.

Examples:

- Improve security posture
- Implement NIST CSF functions
- Strengthen security controls
- Meet compliance requirements

---

## 2. Complete a Risk Assessment

Evaluate threats, risks, and vulnerabilities affecting organizational assets.

Purpose:

- Identify security weaknesses
- Determine which assets are most at risk
- Recommend appropriate security controls

Example findings:

- Weak access controls
- Poor asset management
- Unsecured employee devices
- Inadequate network protection

---

## 3. Conduct a Controls Assessment

Evaluate whether existing controls effectively protect organizational assets.

Questions to consider:

- Are current controls effective?
- Which controls are missing?
- Are administrative, technical, and physical controls sufficient?

---

## 4. Assess Compliance

Verify compliance with applicable laws and regulations.

Examples:

- GDPR
- PCI DSS
- HIPAA
- ISO/IEC 27001

Example:

A company operating in the European Union and processing credit card payments must comply with both **GDPR** and **PCI DSS**.

---

## 5. Communicate Results

Prepare and present the audit findings to stakeholders.

The final report should include:

- Audit scope
- Audit goals
- Identified risks
- Compliance status
- Recommended improvements
- Risk priorities
- Remediation timeline

---

# Audit Checklist

Before conducting an audit:

- Define the audit scope
- List assets to evaluate
- Review policies and procedures
- Complete a risk assessment
- Conduct the audit
- Create a mitigation plan
- Communicate findings

---

# Mitigation Plan

A **mitigation plan** outlines how identified risks will be reduced.

Purpose:

- Lower organizational risk
- Improve security posture
- Reduce legal penalties
- Strengthen security controls

---

# Questions Asked During an Audit

- What is the audit trying to achieve?
- Which assets are most critical?
- Which assets are most at risk?
- Are current controls effective?
- Which compliance regulations apply?
- What improvements should be implemented?

---

# Benefits of Security Audits

- Improve security posture
- Detect vulnerabilities early
- Verify compliance
- Reduce business risk
- Prevent security incidents
- Avoid regulatory fines
- Strengthen organizational security

---

# Key Takeaways

- A **security audit** reviews an organization's security controls, policies, and procedures.
- Audits can be **internal** or **external**.
- Internal audits typically include five major phases:
  1. Establish scope and goals
  2. Complete a risk assessment
  3. Conduct a controls assessment
  4. Assess compliance
  5. Communicate results
- Security controls are grouped into **administrative**, **technical**, and **physical** categories.
- Frameworks such as **NIST CSF** and **ISO/IEC 27001** help organizations prepare for audits.
- A mitigation plan is created to address identified risks and improve the organization's overall security posture.
