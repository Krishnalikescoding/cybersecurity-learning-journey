# Threats, Risks, and Vulnerabilities

An **asset** is any item that has value to an organization. Throughout their lifecycle, organizations acquire many different types of assets, including:

- Physical office spaces
- Computers and other hardware
- Customers' Personally Identifiable Information (PII)
- Intellectual property (patents, copyrights, trade secrets)
- Business data and applications
- Financial records

Organizations operate in environments where these assets face numerous **threats, risks, and vulnerabilities**. Understanding these concepts is essential for protecting organizational resources.

---

# Threats

A **threat** is any circumstance or event that can negatively impact an organization's assets.

Threats may come from:

- Cybercriminals
- Insider threats
- Natural disasters
- Human error
- System failures

## Example: Social Engineering

One common threat is a **social engineering attack**.

**Social engineering** is a manipulation technique that exploits human error to gain unauthorized access, private information, or valuables.

One of the most common forms of social engineering is **phishing**.

### Phishing

Phishing is a technique used to steal sensitive information such as:

- Usernames
- Passwords
- Banking information
- Credit card details
- Personal information

Attackers often send emails that appear to come from legitimate companies or trusted individuals and contain malicious links or attachments.

---

# Risks

A **risk** is anything that can impact the **Confidentiality, Integrity, or Availability (CIA)** of an asset.

A risk represents the **likelihood that a threat will successfully exploit a vulnerability**.

### Example

An organization that does not maintain proper backup procedures is at risk of permanently losing important data during a cyberattack or system failure.

Organizations usually classify risks into different levels based on the value of the asset and the potential impact of a compromise.

---

## Low Risk

A **low-risk asset** contains information that:

- Is publicly available
- Would not damage the organization's reputation
- Would not interrupt business operations
- Would not cause financial loss if compromised

### Examples

- Public website content
- Published research
- Public announcements

---

## Medium Risk

A **medium-risk asset** contains information that is not public and could moderately affect the organization if exposed.

Compromising these assets may:

- Cause financial losses
- Damage reputation
- Interrupt some business operations

### Example

The early release of a company's quarterly earnings before the official announcement could affect the company's stock value.

---

## High Risk

A **high-risk asset** contains highly sensitive information protected by laws or regulations.

Compromise of these assets can severely affect:

- Organizational finances
- Reputation
- Business operations
- Regulatory compliance

### Examples

- Sensitive Personally Identifiable Information (SPII)
- Personally Identifiable Information (PII)
- Intellectual property
- Financial records
- Medical records
- Trade secrets

---

# Vulnerabilities

A **vulnerability** is a weakness that can be exploited by a threat.

A vulnerability alone does not create risk. Both a **threat** and a **vulnerability** must exist for a risk to occur.

## Common Vulnerabilities

- Outdated firewalls
- Outdated software
- Unpatched applications
- Weak passwords
- Unprotected confidential data
- Misconfigured systems
- Poor security policies

---

# People as Vulnerabilities

People can also become vulnerabilities.

The actions of:

- Employees
- Clients
- Contractors
- Third-party vendors

can significantly affect an organization's security.

Mistakes such as clicking phishing links, using weak passwords, or sharing sensitive information may expose organizational assets.

Because of this, cybersecurity is a shared responsibility.

---

# Role of an Entry-Level Security Analyst

Entry-level analysts help reduce risks by educating employees and improving security awareness.

Common responsibilities include:

- Teaching employees how to recognize phishing emails
- Promoting good password practices
- Encouraging employees to report suspicious activity
- Monitoring employee access to critical assets
- Documenting access logs
- Supporting vulnerability management efforts
- Helping improve organizational security policies

---

# Physical Security Measures

Protecting physical assets is just as important as protecting digital assets.

Examples include:

- Employee access cards
- Visitor restrictions
- Secure office entry systems
- Surveillance systems
- Physical access monitoring

These measures help prevent unauthorized individuals from accessing sensitive areas.

---

# Key Takeaways

- An **asset** is anything valuable to an organization.
- A **threat** is any event or circumstance that can harm an asset.
- **Social engineering** manipulates people into revealing sensitive information.
- **Phishing** is a common social engineering attack that steals credentials and personal data.
- A **risk** is the likelihood that a threat will exploit a vulnerability and affect the Confidentiality, Integrity, or Availability (CIA) of an asset.
- Risks are commonly classified as **Low**, **Medium**, or **High** based on their potential impact.
- A **vulnerability** is a weakness that attackers can exploit.
- A risk exists only when both a **threat** and a **vulnerability** are present.
- People can be one of an organization's biggest vulnerabilities, making security awareness training essential.
- Entry-level security analysts help protect organizations by educating users, monitoring access, documenting activity, and reducing vulnerabilities.
