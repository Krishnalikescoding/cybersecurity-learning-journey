# Security Lifecycle, Frameworks, Controls, and Compliance

A **security lifecycle** is a constantly evolving set of policies and standards used to manage security risks and protect organizational assets.

It combines **security controls**, **security frameworks**, and **compliance regulations** to ensure organizations minimize risk and meet legal requirements.

---

# Relationship Between Controls, Frameworks, and Compliance

The **CIA Triad** (Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability) is the foundation of cybersecurity. It helps organizations assess risk when designing systems and creating security policies.

The relationship between these concepts is:

- **CIA Triad** defines the core security principles.
- **Security Controls** are safeguards that protect systems and data.
- **Security Frameworks** provide structured guidelines for implementing controls.
- **Compliance** ensures organizations follow internal policies and external laws.

Together, they help organizations maintain a strong security posture.

---

# CIA Triad

The **CIA Triad** consists of three foundational principles:

- **Confidentiality** – Protects data from unauthorized access.
- **Integrity** – Ensures data remains accurate and trustworthy.
- **Availability** – Ensures authorized users can access data and systems when needed.

---

# Security Controls

**Security controls** are safeguards designed to reduce specific security risks.

Examples include:

- Access controls
- Firewalls
- Encryption
- Multi-factor authentication (MFA)
- Security awareness training
- Antivirus software

Security controls help protect systems from threats, vulnerabilities, and attacks.

---

# Security Frameworks

Security frameworks are guidelines used to build plans that reduce risks to data and privacy.

## Four Core Components

### 1. Identify and Document Security Goals

Determine what the organization wants to protect and what compliance requirements must be met.

### 2. Set Guidelines to Achieve Security Goals

Develop policies and procedures that support organizational security objectives.

### 3. Implement Strong Security Processes

Apply security controls and operational procedures to protect systems and information.

### 4. Monitor and Communicate Results

Continuously monitor security performance and report findings to management.

---

# Compliance

**Compliance** is the process of following:

- Internal organizational standards
- External laws and regulations

Organizations that maintain compliance generally reduce their cybersecurity risks.

---

# National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)

The **National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)** is a U.S. government agency that develops voluntary cybersecurity standards and frameworks used worldwide.

Organizations use NIST guidance to improve security and manage risk.

---

# NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)

The **NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)** provides standards, guidelines, and best practices for managing cybersecurity risks.

Its purpose is to:

- Identify risks
- Protect assets
- Detect security incidents
- Respond to attacks
- Recover from incidents

---

# NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF)

The **NIST Risk Management Framework (RMF)** provides a structured process for managing cybersecurity risks throughout a system's lifecycle.

It helps organizations:

- Assess risks
- Select security controls
- Implement controls
- Monitor effectiveness

---

# Major Security Frameworks and Compliance Standards

## FERC-NERC

**Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC)** and the **North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC)** regulate organizations involved with the U.S. and North American power grid.

Organizations must:

- Prepare for cyber incidents
- Reduce cybersecurity risks
- Report security incidents
- Follow Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP) standards

---

## FedRAMP

**Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program (FedRAMP)** standardizes security assessment, authorization, monitoring, and cloud security for U.S. government cloud services.

Purpose:

- Standardize cloud security
- Ensure secure government cloud services
- Maintain continuous monitoring

---

## Center for Internet Security (CIS)

The **Center for Internet Security (CIS)** is a nonprofit organization that provides:

- CIS Controls
- Security benchmarks
- Best practices

Purpose:

- Protect systems and networks
- Improve organizational defenses
- Guide incident response

---

## GDPR

**General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)** is a European Union privacy law that protects the personal data of EU citizens.

Organizations must:

- Be transparent about collected data
- Protect personal information
- Notify affected individuals within **72 hours** after a data breach
- Respect users' privacy rights

Failure to comply may result in significant fines.

---

## PCI DSS

**Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS)** is an international security standard for organizations that store, process, or transmit credit card information.

Purpose:

- Protect payment card data
- Reduce credit card fraud
- Secure payment environments

---

## HIPAA

**Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)** is a U.S. law that protects patients' health information.

HIPAA consists of three major rules:

- Privacy Rule
- Security Rule
- Breach Notification Rule

Organizations must notify patients if their **Protected Health Information (PHI)** is exposed.

PHI includes information related to:

- Physical health
- Mental health
- Medical treatment
- Healthcare payments

---

## HITRUST

**Health Information Trust Alliance (HITRUST)** is a security framework and assurance program that helps healthcare organizations achieve HIPAA compliance.

---

## ISO

**International Organization for Standardization (ISO)** develops international standards for technology, manufacturing, and management.

ISO helps organizations improve:

- Business processes
- Quality management
- Planning
- Staff retention
- Operational efficiency

---

## SOC 1 and SOC 2

**System and Organization Controls (SOC)** reports were developed by the **American Institute of Certified Public Accountants (AICPA)**.

SOC reports evaluate an organization's controls related to:

- Confidentiality
- Integrity
- Availability
- Privacy
- Security
- Financial reporting

SOC assessments review access controls for different organizational roles, including:

- Associates
- Supervisors
- Managers
- Executives
- Vendors
- Other authorized users

Weak controls can increase the risk of fraud and data breaches.

---

# Additional Regulations to Explore

Some cybersecurity regulations change frequently.

Examples include:

- Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act (GLBA)
- Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX)

Security professionals should stay informed about updates to these regulations.

---

# Key Takeaways

- A **security lifecycle** is an ongoing process of managing cybersecurity risks.
- The **CIA Triad** provides the foundation for organizational security.
- **Security controls** reduce threats and vulnerabilities.
- **Security frameworks** provide structured guidance for implementing security.
- **Compliance** ensures organizations follow internal standards and legal regulations.
- **NIST CSF** and **NIST RMF** are widely used cybersecurity frameworks.
- Important regulations and standards include **FERC-NERC, FedRAMP, CIS, GDPR, PCI DSS, HIPAA, HITRUST, ISO, and SOC 1/SOC 2**.
- Security analysts should stay informed about evolving frameworks, controls, and compliance requirements to help protect organizations and the people they serve.
