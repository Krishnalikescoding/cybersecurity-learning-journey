# Common Cybersecurity Attacks and Social Engineering

Cybersecurity professionals must understand the various attack methods used by threat actors to compromise systems, steal information, and disrupt operations. Among the most common attack categories are phishing, malware, and social engineering attacks.

## Phishing

Phishing is the use of digital communications to trick individuals into revealing sensitive information or installing malicious software. Attackers often impersonate trusted organizations, colleagues, or service providers to convince victims to take harmful actions.

### Common Types of Phishing Attacks

#### Business Email Compromise (BEC)

Business Email Compromise (BEC) is a phishing attack in which a threat actor sends an email that appears to come from a trusted source, such as a company executive, vendor, or partner. The goal is usually to obtain sensitive information or persuade the victim to transfer money.

#### Spear Phishing

Spear phishing is a targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific individual or group. Attackers customize their messages using personal or organizational information, making the communication appear legitimate and trustworthy.

#### Whaling

Whaling is a specialized form of spear phishing that targets high-ranking executives and senior management personnel. These attacks often seek access to confidential business information or financial resources.

#### Vishing

Vishing, or voice phishing, uses phone calls or voice communication technologies to trick victims into revealing sensitive information or performing unauthorized actions.

#### Smishing

Smishing, or SMS phishing, uses text messages to deceive users into sharing confidential information, clicking malicious links, or downloading harmful software.

## Malware

Malware, short for *malicious software*, refers to software specifically designed to harm devices, networks, or users. Threat actors use malware to steal information, gain unauthorized access, disrupt operations, or generate financial profit.

### Common Types of Malware

#### Viruses

A virus is malicious code designed to interfere with computer operations and damage files, software, or data. Viruses require user interaction to spread, such as opening an infected attachment or downloading a malicious file. Once activated, the virus can replicate and infect other files within the system.

#### Worms

Worms are self-replicating malware that spread automatically across networks without requiring user interaction. Unlike viruses, worms do not need to attach themselves to other programs and can rapidly infect multiple devices.

#### Ransomware

Ransomware is malware that encrypts a victim's files or systems and demands payment in exchange for restoring access. These attacks can severely disrupt business operations and lead to significant financial losses.

#### Spyware

Spyware is malware designed to secretly collect information from users without their knowledge or consent. It may gather sensitive data such as emails, passwords, browsing activity, financial information, location data, and personal communications.

## Social Engineering

Social engineering is a manipulation technique that exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities. Attackers use deception, trust, and persuasion to convince individuals to reveal confidential information, grant access, or perform actions that compromise security.

Human error often occurs because people naturally trust others, especially when requests appear legitimate or urgent. Social engineering attacks exploit this tendency.

### Common Types of Social Engineering Attacks

#### Social Media Phishing

Threat actors collect information from social media platforms to learn about their targets. They then use this information to create personalized attacks that appear trustworthy.

#### Watering Hole Attack

In a watering hole attack, attackers compromise a website frequently visited by a targeted group of users. When victims visit the infected website, their systems may become compromised.

#### USB Baiting

USB baiting involves leaving infected USB drives in locations where employees or users are likely to find them. When the device is plugged into a computer, malware is installed automatically or through user interaction.

#### Physical Social Engineering

Attackers may impersonate employees, customers, vendors, or contractors to gain unauthorized physical access to buildings, offices, or restricted areas.

## Why Social Engineering Is Effective

Social engineering attacks are highly successful because they exploit predictable human behaviors and emotions rather than technical weaknesses.

### Authority

Attackers impersonate managers, executives, law enforcement officers, or other authority figures because people are more likely to comply with requests from perceived leaders.

### Intimidation

Threat actors use fear, pressure, or threats to persuade victims to act without questioning the legitimacy of a request.

### Consensus (Social Proof)

People often trust actions that appear to be accepted by others. Attackers exploit this by claiming that coworkers, friends, or other employees have already approved or participated in similar actions.

### Scarcity

Threat actors create the impression that opportunities, products, or services are limited, encouraging victims to act quickly before missing out.

### Familiarity

Attackers build a false sense of friendship or personal connection to increase trust and reduce suspicion.

### Trust

Long-term social engineering campaigns often involve developing relationships with victims over time to gain access to valuable information.

### Urgency

Attackers create a sense of immediate action by claiming that accounts will be suspended, payments are overdue, or security issues require instant attention.

## Defending Against These Attacks

Organizations can reduce the risk of cyberattacks by:

* Providing regular cybersecurity awareness training
* Verifying requests for sensitive information
* Keeping software and systems updated
* Using strong passwords and multi-factor authentication (MFA)
* Monitoring networks for suspicious activity
* Restricting access to sensitive data based on business needs

## Key Takeaways

* Phishing attacks use deceptive communications to steal information or distribute malware.
* Malware includes viruses, worms, ransomware, and spyware, each with different attack methods and objectives.
* Social engineering exploits human behavior rather than technical vulnerabilities.
* Attackers commonly use authority, urgency, trust, and intimidation to manipulate victims.
* Security awareness, strong security practices, and user education are essential for defending against modern cyber threats.
